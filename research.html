Research

Decoding concrete behavior through multi-physics simulation and experimentation

Microscopic interactions to monumental performance

Research Interests

Hydration and Microstructure development

By experimentally understanding the dissolution rate or hydration rate of different components in the cement system, I can capture the microstructural development of the cement paste. This includes the arrangement of different particles in the domain, pore structure, and phase distributions within the cement matrix. We combine diffraction, calorimetry, and imaging techniques to map evolving pore structure. This provides insights for optimizing mix designs and improving the sustainability and resilience of concrete structures.

Reaction transport for Durability

This research theme of reaction–transport simulations investigates the durability of concrete structures. By coupling transport of ions with the chemical reactions within the cementitious matrix, I analyze how aggressive environments affect concrete performance. These simulations capture processes such as carbonation, chloride ingress, and sulfate attack, providing insights into microstructural changes and degradation mechanisms over time.

Poromechanical modeling

My research focuses on poromechanical modeling to understand the internal expansion mechanisms in concrete. We simulate how the formation of different internal expansive species, such as ettringite, ASR products, and corrosion products, generates internal stress. This approach is effective in capturing the development of microcracks and internal stresses in both free and reinforced concrete.

Multiscale modeling of concrete

This research theme focuses on upscaling concrete’s physical properties from the nanoscale to the macroscale. Using advanced analytical modeling, we can predict and upscale key characteristics such as elastic properties, creep behavior, and diffusivity. This approach enables an accurate assessment of concrete performance and optimizes mix design by linking with the hydration and microstructure development models.
